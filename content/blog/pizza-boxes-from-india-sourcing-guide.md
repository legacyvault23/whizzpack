Pizza boxes are one of the highest-volume, highest-frequency consumables in the food service industry. A busy pizzeria can go through thousands of boxes every week. For independent operators, regional chains, and food service distributors, the cost of boxes directly affects the margin on every single order. That is why more and more US buyers are looking to import pizza boxes from India rather than buying through domestic distributors.

This guide explains what that process actually looks like, what you need to specify, what to watch out for, and how to ensure the boxes you receive are food-safe and print-ready.

## Why US Buyers Import Pizza Boxes from India

The US food service industry consumes billions of corrugated boxes annually. Most of them are sourced through domestic distributors, which adds multiple layers of margin between the manufacturer and the end buyer. When buyers go direct to an Indian factory, they cut those layers out entirely.

India has a well-developed corrugated packaging industry with manufacturing concentrated in Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Punjab. Factories in these regions have invested in modern flexographic printing lines, die-cutting equipment, and automated assembly. Many already export to US food chains and regional distributors, so the process is not experimental.

The main reasons US buyers make the switch:

**Volume cost savings.** Indian factories operate at significantly lower labour costs than US domestic manufacturers. On a high-volume SKU like a standard 12-inch or 16-inch pizza box, savings on a per-unit basis add up substantially over a year.

**Custom print flexibility.** If you want your logo, colour branding, or specific messaging on your boxes, domestic distributors often require large minimum orders or long lead times for custom runs. Indian factories typically offer full flexographic print customisation starting from a single container load.

**Consistent specification control.** When you buy through a distributor, the underlying factory can change without notice. Direct import means you control the board grade, flute type, and construction across every order.

## What Specs to Know Before Ordering

Pizza boxes have a narrower set of requirements than general shipping corrugated, but those requirements matter more because food contact is involved. Here is what to specify clearly.

### Board Grade and Flute Type

Standard pizza boxes are made from single-wall corrugated board, most commonly B-flute or E-flute. B-flute (around 3mm thick) gives a good balance of rigidity and stackability. E-flute (around 1.5mm) produces a flatter, smoother surface that takes print well and is lighter to ship.

For pizza boxes, the most important thing is the paper grades used for the liner and medium. A common spec for a US food service box is:

- **Outer liner:** 150 GSM or 175 GSM Kraft
- **Medium (fluting):** 120 GSM or 150 GSM semi-chemical fluting
- **Inner liner:** 150 GSM test liner or white liner if you want a clean interior

If you want a food-grade white interior (which many US buyers prefer for appearance), specify white inner liner explicitly. The exterior Kraft gives you the standard brown look, or you can specify a white top liner for full exterior print.

### Box Dimensions

Pizza boxes in the US are most commonly specified in inches. Standard sizes are 8x8, 10x10, 12x12, 14x14, and 16x16, though custom sizes are available. Specify inside dimensions (ID) rather than outside dimensions (OD), since the flute thickness affects the outside measurement.

Always send a dimensional drawing or a sample box if you have one. A good factory will confirm the cut and crease pattern before production begins.

### Print Requirements

Most Indian pizza box manufacturers use water-based flexographic printing, which is food-safe and produces clean results for 1 to 4 colour jobs. If you are ordering a plain box with no branding, printing is not a variable. If you want custom print:

- Provide artwork in PDF or AI format with 300 DPI resolution
- Specify Pantone colours if colour accuracy matters for brand consistency
- Request a pre-production proof (digital or physical) before the full run begins

### Food Safety and Certifications

For food contact packaging sold in the US market, buyers should ask suppliers for documentation confirming:

- **Virgin fibre content or food-safe recycled content.** Pizza boxes come into contact with food, so the fibre source matters. Some Indian manufacturers use 100 percent virgin Kraft; others use a blend. Ask what the inner liner is made from.
- **FDA 21 CFR compliance.** Paper and paperboard used in food contact applications in the US must comply with FDA 21 CFR Part 176. Request a written declaration from your supplier.
- **Ink safety.** Confirm that printing inks on food contact surfaces are food-safe and do not migrate through the board.

A reputable manufacturer will have these declarations available without hesitation. If a supplier is vague about food safety documentation, treat that as a red flag.

## How Shipping Works from India to the USA

Most pizza box orders are shipped by sea freight in 20-foot or 40-foot containers. Pizza boxes are bulky relative to their weight, so a container fills on volume before it fills on weight.

**Nearest port for Rajkot-based manufacturers:** Mundra Port (INMUN) in Gujarat is the closest major port to Rajkot, approximately 130 km away. Mundra is one of India's busiest container ports with direct vessel services to US East Coast and West Coast ports.

**Well-connected US destination ports from Mundra:**

For US West Coast buyers, Los Angeles (USLAX) and Long Beach (USLGB) receive direct services from Mundra with transit times of approximately 18 to 22 days. For East Coast buyers, New York/New Jersey (USNYC) and Savannah, Georgia (USSAV) are the most efficient endpoints, with transit times of 22 to 28 days via Suez Canal routing.

If you are distributing to the Midwest, Savannah is often preferred because it connects efficiently to rail and trucking corridors into Chicago, Atlanta, and beyond.

**Incoterms to use:** For a first import, FOB (Free on Board) is the most common starting point. The Indian manufacturer loads your goods onto the vessel at Mundra, and your freight forwarder handles everything from there. Once you build a relationship and volume, you can move to CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) if you prefer the supplier to arrange freight.

**Lead time:** A standard production and shipping cycle from India to the US runs 6 to 10 weeks from order confirmation. This includes production (typically 2 to 4 weeks) plus vessel booking and transit. Plan your inventory accordingly, especially around Indian holidays (Diwali in October/November can slow production).

## Minimum Order Quantities

Most Indian corrugated box factories work in full container loads (FCL). A 40-foot container holds roughly 15,000 to 25,000 pizza boxes depending on size and how the boxes are packed (flat-packed vs pre-assembled). A 20-foot container holds roughly half that.

Some factories will also do less-than-container-load (LCL) shipments for buyers testing a new specification, though the per-unit economics are less favourable.

If you are a distributor buying for resale, an FCL order is typically the baseline. If you are an independent pizzeria testing the channel for the first time, look for a manufacturer willing to consolidate your order with others or offer a trial LCL shipment.

## Questions to Ask a Supplier Before Ordering

Before placing a first order, run through this list with any Indian manufacturer you are evaluating:

1. Do you have FDA 21 CFR compliance documentation for your food contact paper?
2. What is the recycled content percentage in your inner liner?
3. What printing method do you use, and are your inks food-safe?
4. Can you provide a physical sample before the production run?
5. What is your quality control process for dimension consistency?
6. Have you exported pizza boxes to the US before? Can you provide a reference?
7. What is your lead time from order confirmation to goods on vessel at Mundra?
8. Do you offer flat-pack or do boxes come pre-assembled?

A manufacturer who has exported to the US before will answer all of these without needing follow-up. The responses will also tell you a lot about how organised their operation is.

## What to Expect on Your First Order

Importing directly from India for the first time involves a few first-time steps that are worth planning for.

**Customs entry.** You will need a US customs broker to clear your shipment. The HS code for corrugated paper boxes is 4819.10. The duty rate for corrugated boxes imported from India is currently 0 percent under normal trade relations.

**Inspection.** You can arrange a third-party pre-shipment inspection (PSI) at the factory before goods are loaded. For a first order, this is worth doing. Companies like SGS, Bureau Veritas, and Intertek offer this service and will check dimensions, print quality, and board strength against your specification.

**Buffer stock.** Because transit times are 3 to 6 weeks, you will need to maintain more inventory than you would if buying domestically. Work backwards from your weekly consumption to calculate how many weeks of stock you need to hold.

Once you have done one successful order, the process repeats itself. Most buyers find that after the first container, the second and third orders are straightforward.
